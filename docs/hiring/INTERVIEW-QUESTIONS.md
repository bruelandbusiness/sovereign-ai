# Interview Questions - Client Support Specialist

Use these questions during the 45-minute final interview. Allocate roughly 3 minutes per question to allow for follow-ups. Score each answer 1-5 using the rubric provided.

---

## Scoring Scale

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Poor -- no relevant experience, unclear thinking, red flags |
| 2 | Below average -- vague answers, limited experience, surface-level understanding |
| 3 | Adequate -- reasonable answer, meets minimum bar, some relevant experience |
| 4 | Strong -- clear, specific examples, demonstrates solid competence |
| 5 | Exceptional -- insightful answer, deep experience, exceeds expectations |

**Hiring threshold:** Average score of 3.5+ across all questions, with no individual score below 2.

---

## Behavioral Questions (1-5)

### 1. Tell me about a time you turned a frustrated customer into a satisfied one. What was the situation, what did you do, and what was the outcome?

**What to look for:**
- Specific example, not a generic answer
- Empathy and active listening in their approach
- Concrete steps they took to resolve the issue
- Measurable outcome (client stayed, left positive feedback, upgraded)
- Ownership -- they did not just pass the problem to someone else

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Cannot provide a specific example |
| 2 | Vague example, no clear resolution or outcome |
| 3 | Decent example with basic empathy and resolution |
| 4 | Strong example showing empathy, creative problem-solving, and positive outcome |
| 5 | Compelling story demonstrating exceptional client relationship skills, measurable positive impact |

---

### 2. Describe a situation where you had to learn a complex product quickly. How did you approach it?

**What to look for:**
- Self-directed learning strategies (reading docs, hands-on testing, asking questions)
- Structured approach vs. random fumbling
- Humility about what they did not know
- Speed of ramp-up
- How they retained and organized what they learned

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | No relevant example or passive approach ("someone trained me") |
| 2 | Some initiative but no clear method |
| 3 | Reasonable approach with some self-direction |
| 4 | Clear structured method, proactive learning, good time-to-competence |
| 5 | Exceptional learning system, demonstrates creating their own reference materials, teaching others |

---

### 3. Tell me about a time you identified a pattern in support tickets and proactively addressed the root cause.

**What to look for:**
- Analytical thinking -- ability to spot patterns across individual tickets
- Proactive behavior -- they did not just keep answering the same question
- Action taken: documentation, product feedback, process change
- Impact of their action (reduced ticket volume, improved CSAT)

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Cannot provide an example or does not understand the concept |
| 2 | Noticed a pattern but did not take meaningful action |
| 3 | Identified a pattern and took basic action (e.g., wrote an FAQ) |
| 4 | Clear pattern identification, meaningful action, measurable reduction in tickets |
| 5 | Systemic thinking, built a feedback loop between support and product, significant impact |

---

### 4. Describe a time you had to work independently with minimal supervision. How did you prioritize your work?

**What to look for:**
- Self-management skills (critical for a remote, first-hire role)
- Prioritization framework (urgency vs. impact, SLA-driven, etc.)
- Communication about blockers and progress
- Ability to make decisions without constant approval

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Uncomfortable without supervision, no prioritization method |
| 2 | Can work alone but struggles with prioritization |
| 3 | Functional independence with basic prioritization |
| 4 | Strong self-management, clear prioritization framework, proactive communication |
| 5 | Thrives independently, sophisticated prioritization, sets own standards and holds themselves accountable |

---

### 5. Tell me about a mistake you made in a support role and what you learned from it.

**What to look for:**
- Honesty and self-awareness (red flag if they say "I don't make mistakes")
- Severity of the mistake and how they handled it
- What they learned and how they changed their process
- Accountability -- they did not blame others

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Cannot name a mistake or blames others |
| 2 | Minor example with limited self-reflection |
| 3 | Honest example with some learning |
| 4 | Meaningful mistake, strong accountability, clear process change |
| 5 | Vulnerable, insightful reflection, implemented systemic change that helped the team |

---

## Technical Questions (6-10)

### 6. A client says their AI-generated Google Ads are spending money but not getting leads. Walk me through how you would troubleshoot this.

**What to look for:**
- Structured troubleshooting approach (not random guessing)
- Understanding of digital ads funnel: impressions > clicks > conversions
- Ability to identify where the funnel breaks down
- Knowledge of common issues: targeting, landing page, budget, call tracking

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | No understanding of digital ads or troubleshooting |
| 2 | Basic awareness but no structured approach |
| 3 | Reasonable approach, checks some correct things |
| 4 | Methodical funnel analysis, identifies multiple potential causes, knows what data to check |
| 5 | Expert-level troubleshooting, considers ad quality, landing page, tracking, attribution, and client-side factors |

---

### 7. You receive an alert that email delivery rates have dropped for one client. What steps do you take?

**What to look for:**
- Knowledge of email deliverability concepts (SPF, DKIM, DMARC, bounce rates, spam complaints)
- Systematic approach: check the email service provider logs, domain authentication, content issues
- Ability to distinguish between provider-side and client-side issues
- Understanding of when to escalate vs. self-resolve

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | No understanding of email delivery |
| 2 | Knows emails can bounce but cannot diagnose why |
| 3 | Checks delivery logs, understands bounces vs. blocks |
| 4 | Checks SPF/DKIM, reviews bounce reasons, checks suppression lists, clear escalation criteria |
| 5 | Deep deliverability knowledge, considers sender reputation, content filtering, engagement metrics |

---

### 8. Explain how you would use Stripe to investigate a client who says they were double-charged.

**What to look for:**
- Familiarity with Stripe or similar payment platforms
- Systematic approach: check payment history, invoice list, subscription events
- Understanding of proration, dunning, and retry logic
- Ability to explain findings to a non-technical client

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | No experience with payment platforms |
| 2 | Basic familiarity but cannot describe a clear investigation process |
| 3 | Can navigate a payment dashboard, check payment history, identify duplicates |
| 4 | Understands subscription billing nuances, can explain proration and retries |
| 5 | Expert Stripe knowledge, understands webhooks, billing cycle edge cases, and how to prevent recurrence |

---

### 9. A client calls and says "nothing is working." How do you approach this?

**What to look for:**
- De-escalation skills -- calming the client before troubleshooting
- Clarifying questions to narrow down the actual issue
- Structured approach to identify what "nothing" means specifically
- Ability to manage expectations while investigating

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Panics, asks no clarifying questions, or becomes defensive |
| 2 | Tries to help but asks poor questions, cannot narrow down the issue |
| 3 | Stays calm, asks reasonable clarifying questions, works toward specifics |
| 4 | Excellent de-escalation, strategic questioning, quickly narrows to specific services and symptoms |
| 5 | Master communicator, combines empathy with efficiency, turns vague complaint into actionable ticket within minutes |

---

### 10. What tools or methods do you use to stay organized when handling multiple support tickets simultaneously?

**What to look for:**
- Specific tools and techniques (not just "I'm organized")
- Prioritization within the ticket queue
- How they track follow-ups and prevent things from falling through the cracks
- Time management during high-volume periods

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | No system, relies on memory |
| 2 | Basic approach (email flags, simple to-do list) |
| 3 | Uses a ticketing system effectively, has a prioritization method |
| 4 | Sophisticated system: tags, SLA tracking, follow-up reminders, priority queues |
| 5 | Has built or improved a support workflow, uses data to optimize their own efficiency |

---

## Situational Questions (11-15)

### 11. It's Friday afternoon. You have 8 open tickets, a new client onboarding call in 30 minutes, and you just received an alert that the AI Phone Agent is down for 3 clients. What do you do first?

**What to look for:**
- Prioritization under pressure
- Service outage takes priority over individual tickets
- Ability to triage: outage > onboarding call > open tickets
- Communication plan for each (quick acknowledgment to ticket holders, escalation for outage, whether to reschedule the call)

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Freezes, works on tickets linearly, ignores the outage |
| 2 | Recognizes the outage is important but does not have a clear plan |
| 3 | Correctly prioritizes outage, has a reasonable plan for the rest |
| 4 | Clear triage: escalate outage immediately, send quick acknowledgments to ticket holders, assess whether to keep or reschedule the call |
| 5 | Adds nuance: checks if outage is spreading, communicates proactively to affected clients, delegates or batches lower-priority work |

---

### 12. A client asks you a question about a feature you have never used and do not understand. What do you do?

**What to look for:**
- Honesty -- does not pretend to know
- Resourcefulness -- checks documentation, admin panel, asks internally
- Communication -- sets expectations with the client ("Let me look into this and get back to you within X hours")
- Speed of resolution

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Makes up an answer or says "I don't know" and stops |
| 2 | Admits ignorance but is slow to find the answer |
| 3 | Honest with client, checks docs, comes back with an answer |
| 4 | Sets clear timeline, uses multiple resources, follows up promptly |
| 5 | Turns it into a learning opportunity, documents the answer for future reference, proactively updates internal docs |

---

### 13. A long-time client on the Empire tier ($12,997/mo) emails saying they are thinking about cancelling because they are not seeing ROI. How do you handle this?

**What to look for:**
- Recognition of the revenue impact and urgency
- Empathy first, then data-driven approach
- Ability to pull and present performance data
- Escalation awareness (this should involve the founder)
- Retention strategies beyond just discounting

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Processes the cancellation without attempting retention |
| 2 | Tries to retain but only offers a discount |
| 3 | Empathizes, pulls performance data, presents it to the client |
| 4 | Loops in the founder, prepares a data-driven business review, proposes adjustments to strategy |
| 5 | All of the above plus identifies the root cause (expectations mismatch, underutilized services, campaign misconfiguration), builds a recovery plan |

---

### 14. You notice that the same 3 clients have submitted tickets every week for the past month about the same type of issue. What do you do?

**What to look for:**
- Pattern recognition and root-cause thinking
- Goes beyond just answering the tickets
- Proposes upstream fixes (product change, better documentation, proactive outreach)
- Data-driven approach (documents the pattern, quantifies the impact)

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Keeps answering the tickets without noticing or acting on the pattern |
| 2 | Notices the pattern but only mentions it casually |
| 3 | Documents the pattern and raises it to the team |
| 4 | Quantifies the impact, proposes specific fixes, follows through |
| 5 | Builds a case for a product fix with data, creates interim workaround documentation, proactively reaches out to affected clients |

---

### 15. You are onboarding a new client who is clearly not tech-savvy. They are confused by the dashboard and seem overwhelmed. How do you adjust your approach?

**What to look for:**
- Empathy and patience
- Ability to simplify without being condescending
- Concrete techniques: slow down, use analogies, focus on what matters to them, follow up with written steps
- Understanding that this client's success is still critical

| Score | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 | Impatient, uses jargon, rushes through the same script |
| 2 | Tries to slow down but does not fundamentally change their approach |
| 3 | Adjusts pace, uses simpler language, checks for understanding |
| 4 | Tailors the entire call to their level, uses analogies, focuses on 2-3 key actions instead of everything, sends a simplified follow-up |
| 5 | Creates a personalized experience, offers additional follow-up sessions, identifies what "success" looks like for this specific client, builds their confidence |

### Decision Framework

| Average Score | Recommendation |
|--------------|----------------|
| 4.0+ | Strong hire -- extend offer |
| 3.5 - 3.9 | Hire -- extend offer with specific development areas noted |
| 3.0 - 3.4 | Borderline -- consider a second conversation or practical exercise |
| Below 3.0 | Do not hire |

### Red Flags (automatic disqualification regardless of score)
- Cannot provide any specific examples from past experience
- Blames clients for problems
- Shows no empathy or patience
- Cannot describe any system for staying organized
- Is dishonest or evasive about past mistakes
- Shows zero curiosity about the product or industry
